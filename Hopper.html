Grace Murray Hopper

Grace Murray Hopper, who worked on multiple major projects in the U.S. Navy and received a Presidential Medal of Freedom posthumously. Like the female computers, she was first a wartime mathematician, with a PhD from Yale. During her earlier years in the Navy, she was part of the Bureau of Ordnance Computation Project, becoming one of the first to program the Mark I computer, which was a major part of the Manhattan project. As WWII came to an end, she subsequently worked on the Mark II and III, influencing the adoption of hardware features such as conditional branching, fundamental to the core logic of computer science. Funnily, she is also related to the birth of the computing lingo “bug”, as the team discovered a moth to be the cause of the machine malfunctioning. Hopper’s journey did not stop here; in 1949, she oversaw programming for the first commercial electronic computer, the UNIVAC. She produced the first high-level programming language and its associated compiler despite scepticism, realising the UNIVAC’s potential in its ability to perform general computational tasks. Her technical vision “regularly preceded hardware capabilities” and is named a pioneer in the use of computing by Dr Robin Burk, a title she deserves.
