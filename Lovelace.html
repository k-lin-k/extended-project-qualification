Ada Lovelace

Ada Lovelace is widely recognised as the world's first computer programmer. An avid translator, she had translated many mathematical papers into English. In 1843, she published a translation of Menabra's paper on Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine, the first (theoretical) computer created; this translation featured her notes, thrice as long as the original, and proposed a program to compute the Bernoulli numbers on this machine. Technically, it may not be completely accurate to call her the first programmer, although her program is the first to be well known; Babbage was likely the first to write programs for his machine. However, her notes and program were crucial as it was much more complex and ambitious than Babbage’s initial visions, with the work being implicit - all done by the engine without the need for human calculations. The program Lovelace wrote demonstrates conditional branching and loops, which are concepts still relevant in modern computing. It was an impressive feat with amazing prescience, having been written long before the rapid development of computing as we know it today. On top of this, Ada’s work ended up becoming the only such paper discussing programming of computers for the next century. Her work proved to be influencial, decades and even centuries after her death; she famously argued that machines cannot think, as they are only able to do what we know how to order them to do. Alan Turing echoed this sentiment, dubbing it “Lady Lovelace’s Objection”. She publicised theories of programming in the very primal stages of both physical and conceptual computing, and can undoubtedly be dubbed as one of the founders of computer science.

Her status in the history of computing does not go without doubt; Dorothy Stein, her greatest critic, even doubts the authenticity of her authorship. Upon investigating Stein’s writing on Lovelace, I found much of this critism and invalidation to be flawed; she argues that Babbage’s attitude towards Lovelace’s theories was negative, and his flattering letters were only an act of politeness. Yet, he has no reason to flatter her, if she was really someone of “little natural talent” as Stein claims. Babbage even mentions in a letter that Lovelace “detected a grave mistake which [he had] made” in computing the Bernoulli numbers, proving her competency in the theories. Stein believes that Lovelace was unpractical with her philosophical views, but without those blocks as the foundation, how could Lovelace have such foresight and continue to have her work influence millions?
