Ironically, the person most widely recognised as the first computer programmer is a woman. This title is often dubbed to Ada Lovelace, who wrote a program to compute Bernoulli numbers in her Notes on Charles Babbage’s Analytical Engine. Technically, it may not be completely accurate to call her the first programmer; Babbage was likely the first to write programs for his machine. This is not to say that her work is any less crucial; it was much more complex and ambitious than Babbage’s initial visions, with the work being implicit - all done by the engine without the need for human calculations. The program Lovelace wrote demonstrates conditional branching and loops, concepts still relevant in modern computing. It was an impressive feat with amazing prescience, having been written long before the rapid development of computing as we know it today. On top of this, Ada’s work ended up becoming the only such paper discussing programming of computers for the next century. She famously argued that machines cannot think, as they are only able to do what we know how to order them to do. Alan Turing echoed this sentiment, dubbing it “Lady Lovelace’s Objection”. An avid translator, she had translated many mathematical papers into English, whilst publishing her notes as well; With this, she publicised theories of programming in the very primal stages of both physical and conceptual computing.

Her status in the history of computing is not without doubt; some even question the authorship of her papers, with Dorothy Stein being her greatest critic. Still, Upon investigating Stein’s writing on Lovelace, I found much of her invalidation to be flawed; she argues that Babbage’s attitude towards Lovelace’s theories was negative, and his flattering letters were only an act of politeness; yet he has no reason to flatter someone of “little natural talent” as Stein claims. Babbage even mentions in a letter that Lovelace “detected a grave mistake which I have made” in computing the Bernoulli numbers, proving her competency in the theories. Stein believes Lovelace was unpractical with her philosophical views, but without those blocks as the foundation, how could “prophets” like Lovelace and Hopper have such foresight?
